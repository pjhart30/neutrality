Background

Before the Internet, the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) was created through the Communications Act of 1934; a group that would play an instrumental role in regulating how the Internet is handled and what's considered fair play and what isn't. Along with it, telecommunication companies were given the common carrier label, granting the FCC increased control in regulating their practices, especially preventing telecommunications monopolies. In 1968, work began on ARPANET, a complex packet-switching network that formed the basis of early Internet. Transmition Control Protocol and Internet Protocol (TCP and IP, respectively) are the standards and models by which information is transmitted on the Internet. IP especially can be seen as an extension of the packet-switching system used by ARPANET since it is a way of explaining how exactly a packet gets transmitted from the sender to the receiver [2].

Because of this idea of packet-switching and a sender and receiver, the Internet in its early years, was seen mainly as just a superior communication avenue to more traditional methods like written communication or even relatively advanced forms like the telegram or radio. Thus, it was viewed as a communication service by the FCC as part of Title 1. Laws protecting net-neutrality, a now infamous term first used by professor of law at UVA, Tim Wu, appeared to be on an upswing when the FCC re-labeled the Internet as a utility under Title 2, giving them increased policing power over ISPs. They also passed the Open Internet Order in 2010 which strengthened the FCC's grip over ISPs in favor of net-neutrality. This only lasted two years, however, before the FCC, backed by newly appointed chairman Ajit Pai, repealed net-neutrality back to the more loosely-regulated laws under Title 1 [1]. This included ISPs no longer being considered as common carriers, a big hit to the FCC's ability to enforce net-neutrality. The Open Internet Order was also discarded for all intents and purposes after the FCC lost a suit to Verizon where the tech giant argued that there was legal backing for the FCC to enact and enforce the Open Internet Order policies.


Net-Neutrality


I think the video above is a great explanation of net-neutrality in layman's terms with an easy-to-follow analogy. I recommend watching that before continuing reading if you are unsure of what exactly net-neutrality means. One of the biggest components of net-neutrality is disallowing throttling by ISPs. Throttling is the act of bottle-necking (or flat-out blocking) service to certain customers or for certain requests. The picture of the funnel below helps illustrate how bandwidth gets allocated before and after throttling. An example of throttling is when Comcast was found to be throttling BitTorrent traffic which led to an extensive battle in the courts with the FCC, one that Comcast ultimately won.

To prevent throttling, many ISPs use over-provisioning. Over-provisioning is where the ISP identifies the peaks and valleys in Internet traffic at various times during the day and allocates more or less bandwidth accordingly. So rather than having to throttle requests during the Super Bowl for example, the ISP will increase the bandwidth during the Super Bowl where millions of people will be taxing the bandwidth while decreasing it in the middle of the night where much fewer users need the bandwidth. This is a great and simple way in my opinion of avoiding net- neutrality issues related to throttling.



Political/Philosophical Views

I'm going to compare two political/philosophical views on net-neutrality: laissez-faire capitalism and socialism. A laissez-faire point of view on net-neutrality would certainly be against intervention by the government in the business practices of ISPs, meaning the ISPs are free to operate however they please regarding the allocation of bandwidth and rate limiting wherever they see fit. The price customers have to pay to use the ISP services is also determined entirely by the free market which in theory would encourage healthy competition between ISPs. Due to the overwhelming barriers to entry in the ISP market with companies needing the wherewithal to support the massive network infrastructure necessary to provide Internet services, these markets often break down into natural oligopolies with a small number of firms controlling nearly the entire market.

On the opposite side, a socialist point of view would encourage heavy government involvement in stopping the ISP market from setting out-of-control prices for Internet use, throttling requests they don't find favorable, and rate-limiting customers who don't pay enough money. Stricter regulation is needed to limit monopolies and the abuse of that position of power by ISPs, which of course ultimately has severe negative ramifications for consumers.


Conclusion

Overall, I tend to side with the socialist point of view regarding net- neutrality. Being a consumer that relies heavily on the services provided to me by ISPs, it would make sense to favor the side that has a greater interest and effort in protecting an impartial Internet. And one that does not cost consumers an exorbitant amount of money. Also, from a fairness perspective, the Internet should be an open network of information where people can access all publicly-available sites equally and without ISPs essentially extorting consumers into paying an arm and a leg for their service when they really don't have any other way to access the Internet. Taking advantage of consumers in this way is highly unethical and should also be illegal. I would liken it to the pharmaceutical companies that control the markets for certain drugs people may depend on to live. While maybe not quite as ominously life-or-death, the Internet has become such an integral part of our lives that ISPs being allowed to operate in a way that supports net non-neutrality and partiality parallels the unethical behaviors of pharmaceutical companies who likewise take advantage of the people who have nowhere else to go for the service they depend on. Net non-neutrality has the potential to drive out people who simply do not have the money for Internet access even more so than already exists. These people are then deprived of the freedom of information that every human should be entitled to. Therefore, we must protect net- neutrality at all costs.

The best way I can think of accomplishing this is by having the government continue to impose strict regulations on ISPs throttling and rate-limiting traffic with the purpose of creating fast or slow lanes for different users. Such actions should be met with harsh punishments by the government under those regulations. I think they also need to promote more competition in those markets, possibly by offering greater incentives for smaller comapnies to expand and subsidize some of their startup costs to help lessen the gap between the smaller companies and the industry giants like Comcast.
