Background

Before the Internet, the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) was created through the Communications Act of 1934; a group that would play an instrumental role in regulating how the Internet is handled and what's considered fair play and what isn't. Along with it, telecommunication companies were given the common carrier label, granting the FCC increased control in regulating their practices, especially preventing telecommunications monopolies. In its early years, the Internet was seen mainly as a superior communication avenue to more traditional methods methods like writing letters, or even relatively advanced forms like the telegram or radio. Thus, it was viewed as a communication service by the FCC as part of Title 1. Laws protecting net-neutrality, a now infamous term first used by professor of law at UVA, Tim Wu, appeared to be on an upswing when the FCC re-labeled the Internet as a utility under Title 2, giving them increased policing power over ISPs. They also passed the Open Internet Order in 2010 which strengthened the FCC's grip over ISPs in favor of net-neutrality. This only lasted two years, however, before the FCC, backed by newly appointed chairman Ajit Pai, repealed net-neutrality back to the more loosely-regulated laws under Title 1. This included ISPs no longer being considered as common carriers, a big hit to the FCC's ability to enforce net-neutrality.


Net-Neutrality


I think the video above is a great explanation of net-neutrality in layman's terms with an easy-to-follow analogy. I recommend watching that before continuing reading if you are unsure of what exactly net-neutrality means. One of the biggest components of net-neutrality is disallowing throttling by ISPs. Throttling is the act of bottle-necking (or flat-out blocking) service to certain customers or for certain requests. The picture of the funnel below helps illustrate how bandwidth gets allocated before and after throttling. An example of throttling is when Comcast was found to be throttling BitTorrent traffic which led to an extensive battle in the courts with the FCC, one that Comcast ultimately won.

To prevent throttling, many ISPs use over-provisioning. Over-provisioning is where the ISP identifies the peaks and valleys in Internet traffic at various times during the day and allocates more or less bandwidth accordingly. So rather than having to throttle requests during the Super Bowl for example, the ISP will increase the bandwidth during the Super Bowl where millions of people will be taxing the bandwidth while decreasing it in the middle of the night where much fewer users need the bandwidth. This is a great and simple way in my opinion of avoiding net- neutrality issues related to throttling.
